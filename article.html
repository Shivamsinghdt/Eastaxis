Will Supply Chains Realign Around the Indo-Pacific?

As manufacturing shifts east, EastAxis scholars examine what a reordered trade map means for policymakers, investors, and regional stability — and why Northeast India's corridors may decide how much of that shift the region actually captures.

Ananya Krishnan
Fellow, Trade & Economic Policy
Daniel Osei
Associate Fellow
Published Jul 10, 2026
Download Full Paper (PDF)

Introduction

For three decades, global manufacturing organized itself around a single node. That arrangement is now being renegotiated. Tariff shocks, rising labour costs on the coast, and a sharpening contest over critical technologies have pushed multinational firms to look for a second — and sometimes a third — production base. Vietnam, Indonesia, India, and Bangladesh have all absorbed part of this movement already. The open question is how much of the next wave lands in Northeast India and the wider eastern corridor, and how much passes it by for want of roads, power, and predictable customs.

This paper takes stock of where firms are actually moving capacity, what is pulling them there, and what would need to be true for the Act East corridor to compete for a meaningful share of that capacity rather than watch it settle permanently in Southeast Asia.

Key Findings

  • Diversification is real but shallow: most relocated capacity remains final-assembly work, while component manufacturing and precision tooling stay concentrated in incumbent hubs.
  • Northeast India's land connectivity to Southeast Asia — through Myanmar and via the Kaladan and trilateral highway routes — remains the single largest unrealized asset in the region's trade geography.
  • Port and rail bottlenecks, not labour cost, are now the binding constraint on whether firms choose Northeast India over coastal alternatives.
  • ASEAN economies that paired special economic zones with predictable single-window customs captured disproportionately more relocated capacity than those that relied on cost advantage alone.
  • Currency and tariff volatility are pushing firms toward "friend-shoring" clusters — groups of allied economies — rather than any single replacement country, which favours regions that can offer several linked, smaller sites.

The Shifting Geography of Production

Firms rarely relocate a factory in one move. What we observe instead is a phased unbundling: final assembly shifts first, chasing tariff relief and labour availability, while upstream component production stays put until the new site proves it can hold quality and delivery schedules. Southeast Asia has spent a decade building the industrial parks, bonded warehousing, and mid-level technical workforce needed to absorb that first wave. The result is visible in customs data across Vietnam, Indonesia, and increasingly Cambodia.

India has captured a growing share of this movement too, concentrated overwhelmingly on the western and southern coasts. Northeast India, despite sharing land borders with Bangladesh, Bhutan, China, and Myanmar, has captured almost none of it — not because labour or land is scarce, but because the logistics cost of moving a container from Guwahati to a deep-water port still exceeds the cost of moving the same container from Hanoi to Los Angeles.

The binding constraint on Northeast India's participation in supply chain diversification is no longer labour cost — it is the price of moving a container the last four hundred kilometres.

Northeast India's Corridor Opportunity

Three infrastructure projects would materially change this calculus: completion of the Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project linking Sittwe port to Mizoram, the India–Myanmar–Thailand Trilateral Highway, and a functioning land customs station regime along the Assam–Bangladesh border that matches the single-window standard already operating in Southeast Asian special economic zones. None of these are novel proposals — each has existed on paper for over a decade. What has changed is the commercial incentive for firms to actually use them once built, given how tight the alternative capacity in coastal Southeast Asia has become.

A functioning corridor would not need to compete with Vietnam or Indonesia on scale. It would need to offer firms already diversifying into South and Southeast Asia a lower-cost way to link an Indian final-assembly site to component suppliers already operating in Myanmar, Thailand, and southern China — turning a border region into connective tissue rather than a periphery.

Risks and Bottlenecks

Political instability inside Myanmar remains the largest single risk to any corridor strategy routed through it, and firms will not commit capital to a route they cannot insure. Border trade policy between India and Bangladesh has also see-sawed with tariff disputes over the past two years, undermining the predictability that firms weigh more heavily than headline cost. Absent a durable resolution on both fronts, Northeast India's corridor advantage will remain theoretical rather than commercial.

Policy Recommendations

Governments and industry bodies engaged in shaping the Act East Policy should prioritize three actions over the next eighteen months:

  • Fast-track remaining civil works on the Kaladan project and publish a firm operational date, giving logistics firms a horizon to plan around.
  • Pilot a single-window customs facility at one Assam–Bangladesh land port, modelled directly on Vietnam's Hai Phong bonded-zone process, before attempting a region-wide rollout.
  • Convene a standing trilateral business dialogue — India, Myanmar (where feasible), and Thailand — focused specifically on component-level supply chain linkages rather than headline trade volumes.

Conclusion

The realignment of global supply chains around the Indo-Pacific is not a question of if but of which regions capture the value being redistributed. Northeast India holds a geographic advantage that no amount of coastal investment can replicate — proximity to Southeast Asia by land. Whether that advantage converts into commercial reality depends on decisions that are, for the most part, still within policymakers' control: finishing the roads, simplifying the border, and giving firms the predictability they have already found elsewhere in the region.
